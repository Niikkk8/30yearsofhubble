 <section id="timeline">
            <h1 class="section_headings">Timeline</h1>
            <div class="timeline_container--wrapper">
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">1989</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">The Space Telescope Science Institute (STScI) and NASA co-host the Next
                        Generation Space Telescope
                        Workshop at STScI. The focus was the science and technical capabilities of an observatory that
                        would
                        follow the Hubble Space Telescope after it was decommissioned, which was estimated at that time
                        to
                        be 2005.
                    </p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">1995-1996</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">An STScI committee recommends a significantly larger telescope capable of
                        observing infrared light.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">NASA selects Goddard Space Flight Center and STScI to study the feasibility
                        of the Next Generation Space Telescope. Three independent government and aerospace teams
                        determine that such an observatory is feasible.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">1997</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text ">NASA selects teams from the Goddard Space Flight Center, TRW, and Ball
                        Aerospace to fine-tune the telescope’s technical and financial requirements.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">1999</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Lockheed Martin, Ball Aerospace, and TRW (also partnering with Kodak and
                        ATK) conduct Phase A mission studies, including preliminary analysis of the design and cost.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2002</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Based on two Phase A studies, NASA selects the design of TRW/Ball Aerospace
                        to continue in Phase B detailed design studies, which examine the performance and cost of the
                        chosen design.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">The telescope is renamed from the Next Generation Space Telescope to the
                        James Webb Space Telescope.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">In Phase B, TRW and Ball are awarded the observatory contract, but changes
                        follow immediately. Northrop Grumman acquires TRW, and works with Ball to develop the
                        observatory during Phases B, C, and D.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">NASA selects the flight science working group and the team responsible for
                        developing the Near-Infrared Camera (NIRCam).</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2004</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Construction begins on certain telescope parts that require extensive,
                        long-term work—in particular, Webb’s science instruments and the 18 segments of the primary
                        mirror.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2005</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">NASA approves the use of the European Space Agency’s Ariane 5 rocket to
                        launch Webb into space.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2006</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">The science instrument teams for the Near-Infrared Camera (NIRCam) and the
                        Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI) pass critical design reviews and initiate construction of the
                        flight instruments. All Webb’s essential technologies are tested successfully under flight
                        conditions.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2007-2008</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">NASA has the Webb mission reviewed by internal and external groups. The
                        internal preliminary design review and external non-advocate review conclude that the plans and
                        designs have reached the maturity needed for NASA to commit to phases C and D, which entail
                        detailed design, procurement, testing, and assembly of the telescope and observatory components.
                        Construction begins in earnest.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2009</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">The Integrated Science Instrument Module (ISIM) structure, built to house
                        Webb'’s four science instruments, arrives at Goddard Space Flight Center for testing.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2010</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Webb passes its mission critical design review, which signifies that the
                        integrated observatory will meet all science and engineering requirements for its mission.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2011</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Webb’s mirrors are completed. They are beryllium coated in a thin layer of
                        gold, and have passed cryogenic testing, which exposed them to the frigid temperatures they will
                        be subjected to in space.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2012</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Goddard Space Flight Center receives two of Webb’s four science
                        instruments, the Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI) and the Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless
                        Spectrograph (NIRISS), as well as Webb’s Fine Guidance Sensor, from the European and Canadian
                        space agencies. Webb’s secondary mirror and the first three primary mirror segments also arrive
                        at Goddard Space Flight Center from Ball Aerospace & Technologies Corp.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Northrop Grumman and partner ATK finish constructing the center section of
                        Webb’s backplane structure, designed to hold the telescope’s primary mirror segments.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2013</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">The two side “wings” of Webb’s backplane structure are completed by
                        Northrop Grumman and ATK. Webb’s two final science instruments, the Near-Infrared Camera
                        (NIRCam) and Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec), as well as the remaining primary mirror
                        segments, are delivered to Goddard Space Flight Center.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2014</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Manufacturing of the spacecraft parts (such as fuel tanks, gyroscopes, and
                        solar panels) begins.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Cryogenic testing of the Integrated Science Instrument Module (ISIM),
                        including all four instruments begins, to demonstrate the performance of the instruments as well
                        as the electronics used to communicate with the instruments.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2015-2016</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Cryogenic testing of the Integrated Science Instrument Module (ISIM) is
                        completed. The 18 primary mirror segments are mounted into the backplane, along with the
                        secondary mirror and support struts. The primary and secondary mirrors are integrated with the
                        aft mirrors and the ISIM to create the unit known as the Optical Telescope Element.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2017</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">The Optical Telescope Element successfully undergoes cryogenic testing in a
                        giant thermal vacuum chamber called Chamber A at Johnson Space Center.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2018</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Following successful completion of its final thermal vacuum test, the
                        Optical Telescope Element is delivered to Northrop Grumman in Redondo Beach, California,
                        bringing all of Webb’s flight components under one roof.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">The first successful communications tests are run from the Mission
                        Operations Center at STScI to the telescope’s spacecraft on the ground in California.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2019</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">For the first time, Webb’s spacecraft element—the sunshield and
                        bus—successfully passes acoustic, vibration, and thermal vacuum tests which simulate the rigors
                        of the launch environment as well as the extreme vacuum of space.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Engineers successfully connect the two halves of the Webb telescope—the
                        Optical Telescope Element and the spacecraft—at Northrop Grumman.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="timeline_container">
                    <h2 class="timeline_headings">2020</h2>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Webb is fully folded for the first time and completes final environmental
                        testing to prove it can withstand the shaking and jostling of the launch environment.</p>
                    <p class="timeline_text">Webb’s sunshield is also deployed for the final time on Earth.</p>
                </div>
            </div>

        </section>
